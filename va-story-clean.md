# Twenty Years Lost: How a YouTube Video Showed Me the VA Was Always There

March 23, 2003, was the day I went over the berm into Iraq after waiting around in Kuwait for a month. I was a 31F, Network Switching Systems Operator, on a small extension node remotely attached to an MP unit. They were kicking down doors, taking prisoners, putting them in razor-wire bullpens in the desert, and shipping them south to Kuwait. We gave them telephone and secure internet access. I turned twenty-one years old in Baghdad.

After a year on the ground, I headed back to Germany carrying something I didn't have a name for yet. I was drinking to blackout. Got in trouble. A lot of trouble. By the time the Army was done with me, I'd been demoted to E-1 and processed out with a General Under Honorable Conditions discharge for patterns of misconduct.

Nobody screened me for PTSD. Nobody told me I might still qualify for VA benefits. What I understood was that a general discharge meant I was done. No VA. No benefits. Nothing earned.

I believed that for twenty years.

## The Years Between

After separating at the end of 2004, I tried to put together a normal life. I used my Hazelwood Act benefits for some classes at junior college and carried a 4.0 GPA for a couple of semesters. But I couldn't hold a job. The drinking didn't stop. The anger didn't stop. I didn't know why I was the way I was — or maybe I did know, but I didn't know there was a word for it, and a system built to help people exactly like me.

I've always been a gearhead. Grew up racing motocross and helping my dad swap motors on Toyota pickups in my grandmother's driveway. So eventually I did what I knew. I borrowed four thousand dollars from my mom, got a shop in Hurst, Texas, and started buying and selling cars and trucks.

For seven years, that shop was my life. I found my niche in 4x4 trucks. I figured out something most people don't see: put a set of BF Goodrich All-Terrains on a stock 4x4 wearing highway tires and the whole vehicle transforms — the look, the attitude, the value. I'd source them with cash off Facebook and Craigslist, fix what needed fixing, detail them, and resell. I was good at it. Made real money doing what I loved.

Then COVID hit and I lost the shop.

## The Video That Changed Everything

Back in 2016, I'd heard somewhere that the VA might help with a drinking problem. I went in once but never really followed through. I still believed disability compensation wasn't on the table for someone with my type of discharge.

Then in 2022, YouTube put a video in my feed from a veteran named Combat Craig. He'd gotten the same kind of discharge — General Under Honorable Conditions — after returning from Desert Storm. And he said something I'd never heard anyone say in twenty years: veterans with a general discharge can still qualify for VA disability compensation.

I binge-watched everything he had. I bought his boot camp course. And I did something I should have done in 2004: I filed an Intent to File with the VA.

I put my entire claim together myself using what I learned from Combat Craig. Filed for one condition — combat PTSD — in March 2023. Had my C&P exam in May. The answer came back: 70 percent.

I knew it should be 100. I appealed immediately.

Second C&P exam in August. That same month, the VA granted 100 percent Permanent and Total. But they started the effective date at the decision instead of backdating to my Intent to File. I appealed again. In March 2024, they backdated my rating to January 30, 2023 — my ITF date.

Three appeals. Six months from first filing to 100% P&T. I did the whole thing myself.

I'm now pursuing a discharge upgrade that's been in process for over a year. The VA has recognized that PTSD can be a mitigating factor in exactly the kind of misconduct I was separated for. The rules and standards have changed. I believe the discharge should reflect that.

## What Stability Made Possible

Here's what I need other veterans to hear: that rating didn't just mean a monthly check. It meant I could finally build something without the ground shifting under me.

I've always been tech-minded even without formal training. After losing the shop, I started teaching myself to build websites using AI tools that didn't exist a few years ago. I built SpotOnAuto.com — an automotive repair resource grounded in real factory service manual data covering vehicles from 1982 to 2013. The kind of information I spent years learning the hard way in my shop, now organized and accessible to anyone trying to fix their own vehicle.

Within two months, the site had 16,000 pages indexed by Google and was pulling nearly 7,000 search impressions a day. That is until I made a rookie mistake that destroyed my rankings overnight — something any experienced developer would have known not to do. I didn't blame anyone but myself. And I didn't quit. I rebuilt it.

I rebranded the site as AllOEMManuals.com and relaunched. It's growing every day. It's cited by AI search tools. It's on its way to becoming something that will supplement my VA compensation. My girlfriend Elise helps run the business side and takes care of me and the house. I registered Widescope Industries LLC as a Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business. I'm building all of this from my house in Streetman, Texas — one I bought with a VA home loan.

I'm not a software developer. I'm a combat tech vet who grew up turning wrenches and loves buying and selling trucks. But the tools exist now for people like me to build real businesses if they have the stability and discipline to sit down and learn and not quit until it works. The Army instilled the discipline. The VA gave me stability. Twenty years late — but it gave it to me.

## To the Veteran Who Thinks They Don't Qualify

No matter what discharge you were given, there is a path to the benefits you've earned. Don't live with the same lie I lived with for twenty years — that the VA isn't for you. Hear me: you are almost certainly wrong.

File an Intent to File today. It costs nothing, and it protects your effective date while you figure out your claim. Watch YouTube videos about VA claims. Combat Craig passed away, but his content is still out there. That was all the info I needed to get started on the right track. But there is tons of good, free information out there. Talk to a Veterans Service Organization. Call the VA. Just start.

I spent two decades struggling with something the VA was built to treat. I lost jobs, relationships, and years I can never get back — not because the help didn't exist, but because nobody told me it was there.

Don't let that be your story too.
